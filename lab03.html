Visualization Critique

Visualization

Critique

Information Resolution

The Beautiful News infographic titled “Fewer Pregnant Women Are Dying - 42% Fewer Maternal Deaths” shares a clear and positive message, but it does not go very deep. I think the visualization does a good job of quickly showing one strong statistic that is easy to understand and remember. However, it does not give much background information. There is no timeline or explanation of what years are being compared, and it does not show the original number of maternal deaths or where the data comes from. Because of this, I do not fully understand how big the change actually is or where it happened. Tufte explains that good visualizations should help viewers see patterns and context, not just a final number. In this case, the simple design makes the message clear, but it also makes it harder for me to fully understand the data behind it.

Effects without Causes

Another issue I noticed is that the infographic shows the effect but not the cause. It tells me that maternal deaths have dropped by 42 percent, but it does not explain why that happened. There is no mention of better medical care, improved access to prenatal care, trained healthcare workers, or public health programs that may have helped reduce maternal deaths. Without this information, the statistic feels disconnected from the real-world efforts behind it. I am shown the result, but I am not told what actually caused the improvement. Because of this, the visualization feels less informative and more like a headline. I think including some explanation would help viewers understand what actions lead to positive change and why this progress matters.

Cherry Picking

The infographic also feels like it may be cherry-picking information. It focuses on one positive global statistic, which makes the situation seem better overall. While maternal deaths have gone down globally, this progress has not been the same everywhere. Some low-income countries still have high maternal death rates, but the infographic does not mention this at all. By only showing the positive number, the visualization encourages viewers to feel optimistic without seeing the full picture. This does not mean the statistic is wrong, but it does mean important information is left out. I think this limits my ability to understand where more help is still needed.

Conclusion

Overall, I think the infographic is visually appealing and easy to understand, but it does not fully meet Edward Tufte’s standards for strong data visualization. The design makes the message simple and positive, which is effective, but it leaves out important details and explanations. I understand that maternal deaths have decreased, but I am left with questions about how, where, and why that change happened. Adding more context, such as timelines, locations, or brief explanations, would make the visualization stronger and more informative.
